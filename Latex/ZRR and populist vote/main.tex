\begin{abstract}


This paper studies whether spatial redistribution can reduce electoral support for populism, using evidence from a tax credit program targeted at rural areas in France. The Zone de Revitalization Rurale (ZRR) program aimed to promote economic development and employment in rural areas through corporate and payroll tax exemptions. The main design compares the evolution of the National Front (FN) vote between municipalities that entered the program in its initial wave and municipalities that entered only after 2004. Initial ZRR entry is associated with a reduction of roughly 1 percentage point in the growth of the FN vote share between 1988 and 2002. Spatial border comparisons around the ZRR frontier yield smaller but consistent estimates of 0.3-0.5 percentage points, though these estimates are more sensitive to specification. The evidence is consistent with the idea that geographically targeted state support can dampen populist voting even when measurable local economic effects are limited. The paper contributes to the literature on the socioeconomic and demographic determinants of populism by studying a large place-based redistribution program in a fine-grained municipal panel.


\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords}: Populism, Inequalities, Redistribution, Place-based policy, France rurality, Difference-in-Differences, RDD.

\section{Concluding remarks}\label{conclusion}

Liberal democracies in the Western world have been facing, in the last few decades, the threatening rise of populism. An expression of discontent against the "establishment", the current political and legal system, and national institutions, reminding to some of the interwar period.\footnote{Philippe Corcuff, Les années 30 reviennent et la gauche est dans le brouillard (2014, Textuel); Farid Abdelouahab et Pascal Blanchard, Les Années 30. Et si l'histoire recommençait ? (2017, la Martinière); Emmanuel Macron, to Ouest-France in November 2018: "The moment we are living through resembles the interwar period."}

Our contribution underscores the potential importance of place-based policies in mitigating reactions of populist voters, while also showing how demanding the identification problem is. The evidence is strongest when it follows the timing of early and later ZRR entry and is supported, more cautiously, by spatial comparisons around the program frontier. These results echo the works of \cite{Rajan} and \cite{polanyi1944}, which remind that economics are "embedded" in communities and that regional interventions can matter politically even when their measured local economic effects are limited.
