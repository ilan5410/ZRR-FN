\subsection{Difference-in-Differences Analysis}\label{did-section}

My primary identification strategy compares the \textit{evolution} of the FN vote share between the 1988 and 2002 presidential elections across two groups of municipalities: those that entered the ZRR program in 1995 and those that entered the program only after 2004. As explained earlier, counties that entered after 2004 did so based on eligibility criteria from the 1999 population census, while no counties were withdrawn from the program even if they no longer met the updated criteria.\footnote{The ZRR classification rosters allow me to verify why the late entrants did not qualify in 1995. Most of them already satisfied the low-density criterion at that time; what they lacked was the demographic and economic decline requirement: their median population change over 1980--1995 was $+3.5\%$, against $-4.6\%$ for the 1995 entrants, and their median share of agricultural workers was substantially lower (14\% versus 21\%). They became eligible once the 1999 census documented their subsequent decline. This implies that the comparison group was, if anything, on \textit{more favorable} socioeconomic trajectories in the pre-treatment period, which reinforces the direction-of-bias argument developed below.} Because both groups were eventually deemed eligible for the program, the late entrants constitute a natural comparison group: they share the structural characteristics that qualify a county for the ZRR, but were not exposed to the program between 1995 and 2004.

Figure \ref{fig:did_FN1988-FN2002} presents the central result of this comparison. The top panel plots the binned FN vote share in 2002 against the FN vote share in 1988, separately for the two groups. Municipalities treated in 1995 sit visibly below the late entrants across the entire range of initial FN support: at any level of the 1988 vote, their FN vote grew less by 2002. The bottom panel shows the same relationship after residualizing both vote shares on a set of pre-treatment controls and department fixed effects; the gap shrinks but remains negative.

Formally, I estimate a first-difference specification:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:did}
    \Delta FN_{id} = \alpha + \beta \, ZRR^{1995}_{i} + \gamma \text{X}_{i} + \eta_{d} + \varepsilon_{id}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta FN_{id} = FN2002_{id} - FN1988_{id}$ is the change in the FN vote share of municipality $i$ in department $d$, $ZRR^{1995}_{i}$ indicates initial entry into the program, $\text{X}_{i}$ is a vector of pre-treatment controls, and $\eta_d$ are department fixed effects. Differencing removes all time-invariant municipality characteristics; standard errors are clustered at the audited canton-cluster level. For communes that are split across multiple 1999 canton rows, or for which the canton bridge has a missing canton code, the data pipeline uses commune-specific cluster identifiers rather than arbitrary first-canton assignment. Table \ref{tab:did_result} displays the estimates using the difference-in-difference specification, the within estimator (identical to the first-difference estimator in a two-period setting), and a specification taking the municipalities that entered the ZRR after 2005 as the reference group. The three estimators are almost equal and indicate that initial ZRR entry reduced the FN vote share in 2002 by approximately 1 percentage point.

Figure \ref{fig:dFN_logpop} examines how the change in FN support varies with municipality size across the two groups. Among the late entrants, the FN vote grew markedly faster in the smallest municipalities, in line with the rural populist surge documented in Section \ref{fn-growth}. Among the municipalities treated in 1995, by contrast, the growth of the FN vote is uniformly lower and almost flat in population size. The treatment gap is thus largest precisely in the smallest rural municipalities --- where the program's incentives were most binding --- and narrows as population increases. This pattern is difficult to reconcile with a size-driven confounder, which would affect small municipalities in both groups symmetrically, and instead points to the program itself dampening the FN surge where it was most exposed.

\paragraph{Why is the effect negative?} The sign and magnitude of this difference deserve scrutiny, because selection into early treatment should, if anything, bias the estimate in the \textit{opposite} direction. Municipalities that entered the ZRR in 1995 were selected precisely because they were smaller, more agricultural, and demographically declining --- the profile of places where, as Figure \ref{fig:pop_vs_FN} shows, the FN vote was growing \textit{fastest} over this period. Left-behind trajectories should therefore push treated municipalities toward larger increases in FN support than the later entrants, generating an upward (positive) bias in $\beta$. That the estimated effect is instead strongly negative suggests that the true dampening effect of the program is, if anything, at least as large as the estimate. The residualized panel of Figure \ref{fig:did_FN1988-FN2002} is consistent with this logic: controlling for the observable characteristics that drove selection reduces the raw gap substantially, and the remaining difference is the component that observable selection cannot account for.

\paragraph{Timing of the effect.} A complementary way to probe the causal interpretation is to examine the full trajectory of the FN vote across elections. Figure \ref{fig:event_study} plots dynamic treatment-year contrasts by election year, relative to 1988. This figure is informative, but it is not a clean pre-trend test. The ZRR was instituted by the 1995 territorial-development law, while the decree defining the zones was issued in 1996; therefore the 1995 presidential election should not be treated as definitive post-treatment evidence. The fact that a negative gap is already visible in 1995 is best interpreted as a timing and selection warning. It shows why the paper cannot rely on a simple claim that the two groups had verified parallel trends. The central estimate is therefore the 1988--2002 change, supported by the direction-of-selection argument and robustness checks. From 2007 onwards --- that is, once the comparison municipalities have themselves entered the program --- the differential vanishes and even briefly reverses, which is consistent with the late entrants experiencing their own exposure to the program but should be read as supporting rather than decisive evidence.

\paragraph{Threats to identification.} This strategy assumes that, absent the program, both groups would have followed parallel trends in FN voting. This assumption cannot be directly validated with the current presidential-election series because there is only one clean pre-treatment presidential election. Three concerns therefore qualify the design. First, selection into the initial wave was based on criteria --- population density, employment structure, share of agricultural workers --- that are themselves associated with the subsequent trajectory of the FN vote; as discussed above, however, the direction of this selection runs against the estimated effect, and the estimates are stable once observables and department fixed effects are included. Second, French rural municipalities experienced substantial socioeconomic change over the period: as the t-tests in Appendix \ref{tab:1988-2002} show, almost all municipality characteristics changed significantly between 1988 and 2002. To the extent that these changes affected both groups similarly --- which their shared eligibility profile makes plausible --- they are absorbed by the differencing. Third, entry into the program after 2004 may not have been fully exogenous if, for example, politically driven manipulation affected the later assignment. These limitations motivate the spatial and sample-sensitivity checks below, but they also require caution: the design provides suggestive causal evidence, not a mechanically verified natural experiment.
