\subsection{Difference-in-Differences Analysis}\label{did-section}

Our primary identification strategy compares the \textit{evolution} of the FN vote share between the 1988 and 2002 presidential elections across two groups of communes: those in the initial ZRR wave, coded in 1995, and later ZRR entrants, first observed from 2005. The 1995 territorial-development law created the program; the decree defining the zones was issued in 1996. The later wave used updated 1999-census criteria, while previously designated cantons were not withdrawn.\footnote{The ZRR classification rosters indicate that many later entrants already met the low-density criterion in 1995 but did not meet the demographic-decline or agricultural-employment condition used for the initial wave. They became eligible after the updated criteria applied to the 1999 census.} The later entrants are therefore an informative but imperfect comparison group, not a mechanically equivalent counterfactual. The design requires that, conditional on the stated controls and department effects, their untreated change in FN support would have been comparable to that of the initial wave.

Figure \ref{fig:did_FN1988-FN2002} presents the central result of this comparison. The top panel plots the binned FN vote share in 2002 against the FN vote share in 1988, separately for the two groups. Municipalities treated in 1995 sit visibly below the late entrants across the entire range of initial FN support: at any level of the 1988 vote, their FN vote grew less by 2002. The bottom panel shows the same relationship after residualizing both vote shares on a set of pre-treatment controls and department fixed effects; the gap shrinks but remains negative.

Formally, we estimate a first-difference specification:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:did}
    \Delta FN_{id} = \alpha + \beta \, ZRR^{1995}_{i} + \gamma \text{X}_{i} + \eta_{d} + \varepsilon_{id}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta FN_{id} = FN2002_{id} - FN1988_{id}$ is the change in the FN vote share of commune $i$ in department $d$, $ZRR^{1995}_{i}$ indicates initial-wave entry, $\text{X}_{i}$ is a vector of pre-treatment controls, and $\eta_d$ are department fixed effects. Differencing removes time-invariant commune characteristics; standard errors are clustered using the audited canton-cluster convention. For communes split across multiple 1999 canton rows, or with missing canton codes, the pipeline uses commune-specific cluster identifiers rather than an arbitrary first-canton assignment. Table \ref{tab:did_result} reports a two-period panel interaction specification; Table \ref{tab:did_main} reports the primary first-difference specifications. The latter estimates associate initial ZRR entry with roughly a one-percentage-point lower increase in FN vote share by 2002.

Figure \ref{fig:dFN_logpop} examines how the change in FN support varies with municipality size across the two groups. Among the late entrants, the FN vote grew markedly faster in the smallest municipalities, in line with the rural populist surge documented in Section \ref{fn-growth}. Among the municipalities treated in 1995, by contrast, the growth of the FN vote is uniformly lower and almost flat in population size. The treatment gap is thus largest precisely in the smallest rural municipalities --- where the program's incentives were most binding --- and narrows as population increases. This pattern is difficult to reconcile with a size-driven confounder, which would affect small municipalities in both groups symmetrically, and instead points to the program itself dampening the FN surge where it was most exposed.

\paragraph{Why is the effect negative?} The sign and magnitude of this difference deserve scrutiny. Initial-wave cantons were selected because they were smaller, more agricultural, and demographically declining --- characteristics associated with rapid FN growth in the descriptive evidence. To the extent that these observed trajectories persist after conditioning, selection could bias the estimate upward, against a negative coefficient. This is a direction-of-bias argument, not a proof that the causal effect exceeds the estimate: unobserved differences could operate differently. The residualized panel of Figure \ref{fig:did_FN1988-FN2002} shows that the raw gap narrows after adjustment but remains negative.

\paragraph{Timing of the effect.} A complementary way to probe the causal interpretation is to examine the full trajectory of the FN vote across elections. Figure \ref{fig:event_study} plots dynamic treatment-year contrasts by election year, relative to 1988. This figure is informative, but it is not a clean pre-trend test. The ZRR was instituted by the 1995 territorial-development law, while the decree defining the zones was issued in 1996; therefore the 1995 presidential election should not be treated as definitive post-treatment evidence. The fact that a negative gap is already visible in 1995 is best interpreted as a timing and selection warning. It shows why the paper cannot rely on a simple claim that the two groups had verified parallel trends. The central estimate is therefore the 1988--2002 change, supported by the direction-of-selection argument and robustness checks. From 2007 onwards --- that is, once the comparison municipalities have themselves entered the program --- the differential vanishes and even briefly reverses, which is consistent with the late entrants experiencing their own exposure to the program but should be read as supporting rather than decisive evidence.

\paragraph{Threats to identification.} This strategy assumes that, absent the program, both groups would have followed parallel trends in FN voting. This assumption cannot be directly validated with the current presidential-election series because there is only one clean pre-treatment presidential election. Three concerns therefore qualify the design. First, selection into the initial wave was based on criteria --- population density, employment structure, and agricultural employment --- that are associated with the subsequent trajectory of the FN vote; the observed direction of selection is informative but does not resolve unobserved confounding. Second, French rural communes experienced substantial socioeconomic change over the period: as the t-tests in Appendix \ref{tab:1988-2002} show, almost all measured characteristics changed significantly between 1988 and 2002. Third, entry into the program from 2005 may not have been fully exogenous. These limitations motivate the spatial and sample-sensitivity checks below, but they also require caution: the design provides suggestive causal evidence, not a mechanically verified natural experiment.
